How these numbers are calculated — hover any column heading or figure for the detail

The trade. A borrower's Aave loan falls below its required collateral ratio. Anyone may repay part of their debt and, in exchange, seize their collateral at a discount — the liquidation bonus. A flash loan funds the repayment, so no capital of your own is needed: borrow the debt asset, repay the loan, seize the collateral, swap it back, repay the flash loan, keep the difference. All in one transaction.

The arithmetic, per row:

borrowed = seized ÷ (1 + bonus)
gross bonus = seized − borrowed
flash fee = borrowed × 0.05%  (Aave v3 premium)
swap cost = seized × 0.45%  (0.30% DEX fee + 0.15% slippage)
gas = 550,000 units × live gas price

net (unopposed) = gross − flash fee − swap − gas
net (competitive) = (gross × 15%) − flash fee − swap − gas

Why the two "net" columns differ, and why the second is the honest one. Liquidations are public and contested. Rival searchers bid against each other in priority fees for the right to be first in the block, and that bidding competes away most of the bonus. The competitive column models keeping 15% of the gross bonus, with the other 85% bid away. The hard costs — flash fee, swap, gas — do not shrink.

Note what dominates. Gas is trivial — typically well under a dollar, and identical whether you borrow $1,000 or $1,000,000, because it is the same computation. The swap cost is the real expense, and it scales with size. Anyone whose mental model is "flash loans are expensive because of gas" has it backwards.

What this table is not. Every row already happened and was already won by a professional MEV bot, usually in the same block the position became liquidatable. A green figure in "Net (unopposed)" is what the winner made, not an opportunity that was ever available to you. This table exists to size the prize and show how little of it survives contact with competition — the 15% capture assumption is an estimate, not a measurement.
